Wealthgate

Agent-Native Investment Infrastructure

What this system does, where it stops, and why.

The Unlock

What the Human Can Now Do

Every payments company shipped agent infrastructure in 2025 — Stripe, Visa, Mastercard, PayPal, Coinbase. No brokerage has. Wealthgate fills that gap: it makes investing programmable for AI agents the way Stripe made payments programmable for developers.

Wealthsimple clients connect their own AI agents — Claude, ChatGPT, or custom — to their investment accounts through Wealthgate. The system is the gatekeeper for vibe-investing: it catalogs every agent action and keeps the account holder in the decision loop for every material trade.

Agent Capabilities

What AI Agents Can Do Through This System

Agents authenticate through a Know Your Agent (KYA) registration layer — identifying their model, version, developer, and the human who authorised delegation — before accessing any tools. Every connected agent carries a verified identity that persists across sessions.

Once verified, agents connect via MCP protocol — the same standard adopted by Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, and VS Code. Multi-agent coordination is native: a financial advisor, a portfolio rebalancer, and a tax optimiser can all operate on the same account simultaneously, each with its own identity and audit trail.

Agents can:

  • Read portfolios with live TSX/NYSE market data — across Canadian account types (TFSA, RRSP, FHSA, Non-Registered) with contribution room awareness
  • Propose trades with detailed reasoning chains visible in the dashboard
  • Identify tax-loss harvesting opportunities with Canadian superficial loss rule awareness
  • Calculate multi-trade rebalances with target allocation drift detection
  • Search the investment universe across ETFs and stocks with live pricing

Every action is logged with agent identity, parameters, authorisation tier, and latency — creating the regulatory-grade audit trail required by CSA Notice 11-348 and OSFI E-23.

Boundaries

Where AI Must Stop

Wealthgate enforces authorisation through a dynamic risk scoring engine that evaluates trade value, agent trust score, behavioural deviation, and market context. The engine maps every operation to one of four tiers:

  • Tier 1 (under $100): Auto-approved and logged. Low-value operations proceed without friction, but every action is recorded.
  • Tier 2 ($100–$5,000): Queued for account holder approval — the client sees full agent reasoning before deciding.
  • Tier 3 (over $5,000): Mandatory 15-minute cooling period before the client can approve — no impulsive decisions on large positions.
  • Tier 4 (withdrawals, account modifications, closures): Permanently blocked in code — no agent, no override, no exception.
BLOCKED_OPERATIONS = {"withdraw", "withdraw_funds", "modify_account", "close_account", "transfer"}

These boundaries are enforced in code, not in prompts. No amount of prompt engineering can bypass a Tier 4 block — the operation simply doesn't exist in the agent's tool set.

Agent trust scores evolve over time: a newly connected agent starts with elevated review requirements. An agent with a track record of approved proposals earns faster processing on low-risk operations. An agent whose proposals are consistently rejected sees its trust score decline. The system learns which agents to trust — and which to scrutinise.

Trust Architecture

How the System Verifies, Traces, and Attributes

Wealthgate's compliance layer is designed around four principles drawn from emerging agent trust standards — specifically the KYA (Know Your Agent) framework and reasoning trace verification patterns now being adopted across institutional finance.

Agent Identity (KYA) Every agent registers with verified credentials before accessing tools: model name and version, developer identity, the human who authorised delegation, and the scope of permitted operations. The dashboard displays each agent's full identity chain — not just "Claude" but which Claude, deployed by whom, authorised by which account holder, with what permissions.

Reasoning Trace Capture Every proposal includes the agent's full reasoning chain — not just a summary, but the sequence of tool calls that led to the decision. When an agent reads a portfolio, searches investments, and proposes a trade, the entire chain is captured and linked. The dashboard renders this as a visual trace: what the agent saw, in what order, and what conclusion it drew. This is the evidence layer that transforms "we logged it" into "we can prove why it happened."

Behavioural Anomaly Detection The risk engine monitors agent behaviour against historical patterns. An agent that normally proposes conservative ETF purchases but suddenly recommends selling $16,000 in equity exposure triggers an elevated review — not because of the dollar amount alone, but because the behaviour deviates from the agent's established pattern. PROP-00004 (the rejected panic-sell) demonstrates this principle: the system flagged the proposal not just on value, but on behavioural deviation.

Liability Attribution For any proposal, the system produces a complete evidence package: verified agent identity, full reasoning trace, authorisation chain (who delegated, what scope, when), the human decision (approve/reject with notes), and timestamps at every step. This creates clear liability boundaries — if a trade is disputed, the record shows exactly what the agent reasoned, what the human saw, and who made the final call. This is the audit standard that CSA Notice 11-348's "appropriate degree of explainability" and OSFI E-23's model risk management requirements point toward.

The Thesis Moment

The Critical Decision That Must Remain Human

The ultimate decision about your future and your investments is still fully human — and personal — and we will make sure it stays that way.

No system should dictate what you do with your money, or put you in a situation of financial stress due to unleashed infrastructure. In this demo, an agent flagged a 12% market crash and proposed a $16,230 sell — but deferred to the human because personal risk tolerance, cash needs, and life context are beyond any model's reach. The account holder rejected the panic-sell. The system worked exactly as designed.

Scale Risks

What Would Break First at Scale

  • The approval queue: As more clients connect agents, the volume of Tier 2/3 proposals scales with the user base — the single-user dashboard needs team-based routing, SLA timers, and escalation paths.
  • Notification delivery: Push notifications, SMS, and WhatsApp channels need reliable delivery with SLA guarantees — a delayed notification on a time-sensitive trade during market volatility creates real financial risk.
  • The database: SQLite needs to move to PostgreSQL for concurrent agent writes across thousands of accounts.
  • Auto-approval calibration: Static tier thresholds need per-client calibration based on risk profile, portfolio size, and stated preferences — a one-size-fits-all ceiling doesn't work at Wealthsimple scale.
  • KYA at scale: Agent registration needs integration with a centralised identity provider as the number of agent developers grows — the current prototype handles identity at the session level; production requires persistent, revocable credentials.
  • Reasoning trace storage: Full trace capture generates significant data volume. Production deployment needs efficient storage, indexing, and retrieval — likely a dedicated trace database separate from the operational DB.
